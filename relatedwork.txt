Research Report: Mechanistic Interpretability and Representational Alignment Across Sequence ArchitecturesExecutive SummaryThe intersection of mechanistic interpretability and cross-architecture representational alignment represents a critical frontier in modern deep learning. Historically, the field of interpretability has bifurcated along architectural lines. The interpretability of recurrent neural networks, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs), has predominantly been analyzed through the lens of formal language theory and automata, assessing their capacity to simulate counter machines or pushdown automata. Conversely, the contemporary wave of mechanistic interpretability has been disproportionately optimized for the Transformer architecture, focusing on reverse-engineering attention heads, tracking residual stream communication, and isolating localized circuits. As the computational landscape broadens, a fundamental theoretical question emerges: do mathematically distinct architectures converge upon isomorphic geometric representations when subjected to identical optimization pressures, or do their inherent structural inductive biases force strictly orthogonal computational solutions?An exhaustive review of the extant literature confirms that no prior work currently executes cross-architecture Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) heatmaps across multiple controlled synthetic tasks—specifically modular addition, Dyck-1, Dyck-2, induction, and associative recall—for matched-capacity Transformer versus recurrent models. The literature contains robust methods for reverse-engineering isolated Transformer circuits, behavioral proofs regarding the fundamental limitations of recurrent neural networks on recall tasks, and advanced metrics for measuring geometric vector similarity. However, a controlled empirical synthesis bridging these domains to map the internal representational geometry of attention versus recurrence remains absent. This report establishes the theoretical and methodological topography of the field, isolating the foundational literature, identifying nearest-neighbor studies, and providing a comprehensive framework to position novel research regarding cross-architecture similarity, alignment, and inductive bias.Structured Literature ReviewThe relevant literature can be systematically categorized into distinct thematic domains. This stratification clarifies the distinction between papers that interpret a single architecture, those that compare architectures behaviorally without mechanistic insight, and those that utilize geometric similarity metrics.Mechanistic Interpretability in TransformersMechanistic interpretability within Transformers seeks to reverse-engineer the exact algorithms implemented by self-attention mechanisms and multi-layer perceptrons. The core question animating this domain is how individual components act causally to produce emergent capabilities. Methodologically, this area relies heavily on activation patching, causal scrubbing, and the analysis of simplified "toy" models trained on constrained data distributions. The most relevant findings characterize the residual stream as a central communication channel where attention heads read and write information independently, creating highly specialized circuits such as "previous token heads" and early induction machinery.Furthermore, detailed investigations into algorithmic grokking have revealed that Transformers can learn continuous, periodic Fourier features to solve discrete mathematical tasks like modular addition. These models compute arithmetic by embedding numbers on a circle and utilizing trigonometric identities to rotate representations in the latent space. This body of work connects directly to cross-architecture studies by defining the mechanistic baseline of the Transformer. However, single-architecture studies are inherently limited; discovering a Fourier-based clock algorithm in a Transformer does not explain whether an LSTM solving the same task utilizes an analogous geometric manifold or relies on a vastly different strategy dictated by its recurrent inductive bias.Mechanistic and Behavioral Interpretability in RNNs, LSTMs, and GRUsThe interpretability of recurrent architectures has historically centered on their equivalence to formal computational models. The core question evaluates the computational limits of recurrent state tracking. Methods typically involve formal language theory, analyzing the dynamics of hidden states as models process strings of varying complexity. Foundational findings demonstrate that LSTMs effectively function as counter machines, leveraging their distinct cell states and gating mechanisms (forget, input, output) to track hierarchical depth. GRUs, which simplify the architecture by merging the cell state and hidden state into a single stream controlled by reset and update gates, train faster but frequently struggle with deep hierarchical nesting and dynamic counting compared to LSTMs.Recent literature has attempted to bridge the interpretability gap by applying techniques designed for Transformers, such as contrastive activation addition and the tuned lens, to modern recurrent architectures like Mamba and RWKV. These behavioral and state-tracking analyses provide the necessary architectural contrast for representation studies. If an LSTM is mathematically predisposed to act as a counter machine on tasks like Dyck-2, and a Transformer relies on sparse top-of-stack attention routing, the resulting geometric representations should theoretically diverge, a hypothesis that requires validation through cross-architecture similarity metrics.Representation Similarity MethodsTo objectively quantify whether two disparate architectures have learned the same underlying feature space, the field relies on representational similarity analysis. The central challenge is measuring alignment when networks feature entirely different weight matrices, dimensionalities, and optimization trajectories. Foundational methods include Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) and Singular Vector CCA (SVCCA), though these are limited when the dimensionality of the representations exceeds the number of data points. Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) has subsequently emerged as the standard metric, utilizing the Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion to reliably identify correspondences between layers of networks trained from different initializations.Recent methodological advancements highlight that CKA and Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA) can be confounded by the population structure of data items in the input space, leading to spuriously high similarity scores even among randomly initialized networks. Deconfounding methods, such as covariate adjustment regression, have been introduced to isolate true functional similarity from dataset artifacts. Studies utilizing these metrics demonstrate that architectural family—such as convolutional networks versus vision transformers—serves as a primary determinant of representational similarity, forming distinct topological clusters. For cross-architecture sequence studies, CKA provides the essential mathematical tooling to translate abstract hidden states into comparable geometric heatmaps.Synthetic Algorithmic Tasks for Internal ComputationSynthetic algorithmic tasks eliminate the statistical noise of natural language, providing a controlled laboratory to isolate specific computational primitives. The core question is how sequence models execute fundamental operations like algebraic generalization, memory retrieval, and hierarchical parsing. Methodologically, datasets are meticulously generated to control sequence length, token frequency, and structural complexity, utilizing tools grounded in formal language theory.Tasks heavily utilized in the literature include modular arithmetic, which tests the acquisition of algebraic ring structures; Dyck languages, which test the implementation of pushdown automata logic; and associative recall, which tests key-value memory binding. These tasks are vital for comparative studies because they offer explicit, unambiguous ground truths against which internal mechanisms can be measured. Matching parameter capacity across models on these tasks ensures that any observed differences in performance or representation stem solely from structural inductive bias rather than brute-force memorization capacity.Induction, Associative Recall, and In-Context LearningAssociative recall and induction form the bedrock of in-context learning. The core research question involves understanding how models map prior context to future predictions. Methods generally involve training models on random token sequences punctuated by repeated n-grams, subsequently tracking attention patterns or hidden state activations. Mechanistic analyses prove that Transformers excel at this via the explicit routing of information between a "previous token head" and an "induction head".Conversely, theoretical and empirical analyses demonstrate that recurrent neural networks, constrained by a fixed-size memory state, suffer a fundamental representation bottleneck on tasks requiring perfect in-context retrieval. Even when augmented with chain-of-thought prompting, constant-memory RNNs fail to match the associative recall capabilities of shallow Transformers. Furthermore, hybrid models attempting to combine the state-tracking strengths of LSTMs with the recall strengths of Transformers frequently fail due to shortcut learning, wherein the attention mechanism bypasses recurrent processing. This behavioral dichotomy provides a rich foundation for geometric analysis, predicting a severe representational divergence between architectures on associative recall tasks.Formal Language and Compositional GeneralizationDyck languages, comprising sequences of balanced brackets, serve as the definitive benchmark for hierarchical structure processing. The core inquiry is determining whether a network learns the underlying generative grammar or relies on superficial, continuous heuristics. Methodologies involve training models on Dyck-1 (single bracket type) or Dyck-2 (multiple bracket types) and employing circuit complexity theory or activation probing.LSTMs successfully process these formal languages by co-opting their cell states into dynamic counting mechanisms. Recent mechanistic interpretability studies reveal that Transformers approach the Dyck-2 task via sparse top-of-stack attention routing, where closing brackets attend almost exclusively to the corresponding opening bracket. Crucially, capacity-matched ablation studies on state-space models demonstrate that adding parameter capacity without structural inductive bias fails to improve Dyck-2 recognition, proving that deep nesting requires specific architectural constraints. These findings emphasize the necessity of separating parameter count from structural bias in comparative evaluations.Cross-Architecture Comparison PapersA burgeoning sector of the literature attempts to unify representation learning across diverse models, asking whether different architectures converge on a shared statistical model of reality. This is often framed as the Representational Alignment Hypothesis. Methods heavily feature cross-model CKA, topological data analysis, and model stitching.Recent investigations have mapped the representational flow between Transformers and State-Space Models processing natural language, noting that architectural design natively drives over-smoothing in Transformers, whereas training dynamics drive similar phenomena in state-space models. Other studies have tracked the "convergent evolution" of number representations, showing that while Transformers, Linear RNNs, and LSTMs all develop periodic Fourier spikes for modular arithmetic, LSTMs often fail to structure these features into geometrically separable, linearly decodable manifolds. These cross-architecture studies are the nearest neighbors to the proposed research, yet they uniformly lack a comprehensive, multi-task geometric mapping of classic RNNs versus Transformers.Gaps in the LiteratureA synthesis of the preceding domains highlights a distinct, exploitable gap. The field possesses highly refined methodologies for reverse-engineering individual Transformer circuits on synthetic tasks. The field possesses rigorous theoretical proofs regarding the behavioral limitations of recurrent networks on associative recall and formal languages. The field possesses sophisticated metrics, such as CKA, for quantifying geometric similarity across vector spaces. However, the literature lacks a unified empirical study that synthesizes these pillars.Specifically, no prior work generates cross-architecture CKA heatmaps across a controlled, multi-task algorithmic suite (modular addition, Dyck-1, Dyck-2, induction, associative recall) for matched-capacity Transformer, LSTM, and GRU models. Existing comparative works either evaluate vision models, compare representations using singular probing techniques rather than holistic geometric alignment, or focus purely on behavioral outcomes without inspecting the internal manifold. This gap presents a significant opportunity to visually and quantitatively define how inductive bias shapes the topology of learned algorithms.Critical Analysis of Relevant Prior WorkThe following analysis details the most relevant prior literature, providing comprehensive summaries, contextualizing their placement within the broader research domains, and explicitly defining their relevance and topology relative to the study of cross-architecture mechanistic interpretability.Similarity of Neural Network Representations Revisited
Kornblith, S., Norouzi, M., Lee, H., & Hinton, G. (ICML, 2019).
This foundational paper evaluates methods for comparing neural network representations, identifying the severe limitations of Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) when the dimensionality of representations exceeds the number of data points. To resolve this, the authors introduce Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA), a similarity index capable of reliably identifying correspondences between the hidden layers of neural networks trained from different initializations and with varying widths. Through extensive empirical validation on convolutional networks, CKA is proven to be invariant to orthogonal transformations while accurately capturing the underlying geometric structure of the latent space. This paper belongs in the "Representation similarity methods" section. Its exact relevance is absolute; it provides the mathematical formulation and justification for the core methodology (CKA heatmaps) utilized to compare architectures. It is a strictly foundational text that defines the measurement apparatus.In-context Learning and Induction Heads
Olsson, C., Elhage, N., Nanda, N., et al. (Transformer Circuits Thread, 2022).
This highly influential study posits that the "induction head" circuit constitutes the primary mechanism for the majority of in-context learning within large Transformer models. By defining induction heads in terms of their behavior on repeated copies of random sequences, the authors demonstrate that a phase change occurs during training wherein models simultaneously acquire induction heads and the ability to perform generalized pattern completion. The mechanism relies on a composition of attention heads across different layers, specifically a "previous token head" forwarding information to an "induction head." This paper belongs in the "Induction, associative recall, and in-context learning" section. Its relevance lies in defining the exact mechanical solution Transformers utilize for associative recall and induction tasks. It is a foundational text that provides the baseline expectation for Transformer behavior, allowing the proposed research to ask whether RNNs achieve similar task performance by forming functionally analogous geometric structures despite lacking literal attention heads.Grokking Modular Arithmetic in Transformers
Nanda, N., Chan, L., Lieberum, T., Smith, J., & Steinhardt, J. (ICLR, 2023).
This research utilizes mechanistic interpretability to reverse-engineer how small, one-layer Transformers trained on modular arithmetic tasks transition from memorization to generalization (grokking). The authors uncover a highly interpretable algorithm wherein the network utilizes discrete Fourier transforms and trigonometric identities to convert modular addition into continuous rotations around a circular geometric manifold. This paper belongs in both the "Mechanistic interpretability in Transformers" and "Synthetic algorithmic tasks" sections. It is directly comparable in its use of the modular addition task and its focus on internal representation geometry. However, it is distinctly separated from cross-architecture studies, as it focuses exclusively on the Transformer, creating a gap that requires assessing whether LSTMs and GRUs learn this same Fourier-based clock algorithm.RNNs are not Transformers (Yet): The Key Bottleneck on In-context Retrieval
Arora, S., et al. (ICLR, 2024).
This theoretical and empirical investigation identifies a severe representation gap between Recurrent Neural Networks and Transformers concerning in-context retrieval tasks. The authors mathematically prove that constant-memory RNNs lack the expressive power to perfectly retrieve information from long contexts, fundamentally limiting their performance on tasks such as associative recall and hierarchical graph evaluation, even when augmented with chain-of-thought prompting. This paper belongs in the "Induction, associative recall, and in-context learning" section. It is directly comparable to the proposed research as it explicitly compares RNNs and Transformers on algorithmic tasks. However, its analysis is predominantly behavioral and theoretical; it defines why RNNs fail at recall, while the proposed research will utilize CKA heatmaps to visualize exactly how the RNN's internal vector space collapses or diverges from the Transformer's successful representation during the task.Convergent Evolution: How Different Language Models Learn Similar Number Representations
Zhou, E., Roll, N., et al. (arXiv, 2025).
Investigating the phenomenon of representational alignment, this study demonstrates that language models trained on natural text converge upon periodic, base-10 Fourier features for number representations. Crucially, the authors discover a two-tiered hierarchy: while Transformers, Linear RNNs, and classical LSTMs all exhibit spectral convergence (Fourier spikes), only Transformers and Linear RNNs achieve geometric convergence, organizing these features into linearly separable residue classes for modular arithmetic. This paper belongs in the "Cross-architecture comparison papers" section. It is the nearest neighbor and most directly comparable paper to the proposed research. It evaluates both attention and recurrent models on modular arithmetic to map representational alignment. The critical divergence is methodological; this prior work relies heavily on linear probing and Fourier spectral analysis, whereas the proposed research introduces a systematic, layer-wise CKA heatmap comparison across a broader suite of five syntactic and algorithmic tasks under strict matched-capacity constraints.Delayed Attention Training Improves Length Generalization in Transformer–RNN Hybrids
Phan, B., Ebrahimi, R., et al. (NeurIPS Workshop, 2025).
This paper addresses the complementary strengths of different sequence models, noting that recurrent networks inherently excel at state-tracking (necessary for arithmetic and hierarchical logic) but fail at associative recall, whereas Transformers demonstrate inverse capabilities. The authors evaluate hybrid models combining both mechanisms, discovering that the attention mechanism often causes the network to rely on shortcut heuristics, bypassing the recurrent state-tracking and ruining length generalization. This paper belongs in the "Cross-architecture comparison papers" section. It is peripheral but highly relevant for framing architectural inductive bias. It provides robust behavioral evidence that LSTMs and Transformers prefer fundamentally different computational paths, justifying the necessity of CKA heatmaps to map these differences geometrically.Deconfounded Representational Similarity for Neural Networks
Ding, Y., et al. (NeurIPS, 2022).
This methodological study addresses a significant flaw in standard representation similarity metrics like CKA and RSA: they are easily confounded by the inherent population structure and inter-example similarities within the input dataset. This confounding factor frequently leads to spuriously high similarity scores even between completely random neural networks. The authors propose a covariate adjustment regression technique to regress out the input-space confounder, yielding a metric that accurately reflects true functional similarity. This paper belongs in the "Representation similarity methods" section. It is foundational, providing critical nuance to the application of CKA. Any rigorous study utilizing CKA heatmaps across architectures must address or reference this confounding issue to ensure that high similarity scores reflect true algorithmic alignment rather than mere dataset artifacts.On the Expressive Power of RNNs and Transformers on Formal Languages
Merrill, W. (BlackboxNLP, 2019).
Analyzing neural architectures through the lens of formal language theory and circuit complexity, this paper establishes theoretical upper bounds on the computational power of sequence models. It demonstrates that while LSTMs naturally simulate linear computation graphs suitable for counter automata, Transformers—due to their constant-depth computation graphs—must rely on massive parallel processing and highly specific counting mechanisms to recognize context-free languages like k-Dyck. This paper belongs in the "Formal language / Dyck language studies" section.
